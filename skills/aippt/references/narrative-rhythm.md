# Narrative Rhythm

Use this file in **Stage 3** when shaping the outline and again in **Stage 8** when reviewing the whole deck.

The purpose of narrative rhythm is to stop a solid research deck from turning into a monotonous chain of dense proof slides.

## Rhythm goals

- alternate pressure and release
- keep the main story moving forward instead of repeating the same proof pattern
- make room for orientation, evidence, and conclusion
- prevent layout repetition from flattening the deck

## Healthy sequencing patterns

Good deck flows often look like:

- `anchor -> bridge -> proof -> proof -> breathing -> proof -> closing`
- `anchor -> bridge -> proof cluster -> breathing -> proof cluster -> closing`
- `anchor -> proof -> bridge -> proof -> breathing -> closing`

These are not rigid formulas. They are reminders that dense pages need contrast around them.

## Signs the rhythm is failing

- 3 or more dense proof slides appear in a row with no pacing change
- the same layout repeats on several adjacent content pages
- every slide tries to win with metrics and nothing resets attention
- the closing page feels like “one more proof slide” instead of a resolution
- the table of contents or bridge slides do not actually help orientation

## Pacing heuristics

- after 2 evidence-heavy pages, check whether the audience needs a breathing or bridge page
- use `breathing` slides to simplify, recap, reframe, or show one memorable visual
- use `bridge` slides to turn the page from one section of logic to the next
- reserve the strongest visual emphasis for the cover, one or two anchor proof slides, and the close

## Layout rhythm heuristics

- avoid the same layout on 3 adjacent content slides
- a deck can repeat layout families, but repetition should feel intentional rather than automatic
- when one proof slide is dashboard-heavy, consider a more focused layout for the next proof slide
- dense grids should be balanced by at least one focused or media-led page family in the same section

## Review checklist

Before approving the outline or final review, ask:

1. Is the audience re-oriented after each section shift?
2. Is there at least one breathing point in each dense section?
3. Does the deck escalate toward a conclusion rather than stall in the middle?
4. Do cover, key proof pages, and closing feel distinct from one another?
5. Would a first-time viewer understand why each slide exists without hearing the presenter explain the structure?
